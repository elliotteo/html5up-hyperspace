Hypoxic-Ischemic Encehphalopathy

The Problem

Neonatal hypoxic-ischaemic encephalopathy (HIE) is a clinical syndrome that results from a deficit in oxygen and blood supply to the brain in the perinatal period. HIE occurs in approximately 2-6 per 1,000 live births in Australia and costs the healthcare system approximately $30 million per year. This group of babies make up approximately 14% of the survivors with cerebral palsy. Economic estimates of the total cost to the Australian economy of the survivors with cerebral palsy (CP) who had HIE amounted to $725 million in 2018, approximately $145,000 per person. Moreover, the total cost of lost-wellbeing in these individuals was estimated at $301 million, or $60,500 per person. Together, these are compelling emotional and economic reasons for the development of effective therapeutic interventions.

The cellular stress caused by the impairment of blood and oxygen perfusion is most severe in areas of high energy demand and areas of low perfusion. In moderate and severe cases, serious complications arise.
